Methodology · How The Model Works

The honest read.
Explained.

FieldCheck IQ scores athletes from the public record. Five layers. Eight data source categories. One reconciliation engine that resolves contradictions, weights sources, and assigns confidence. No payola. No star ratings. No coach preferences. Just the evidence, read carefully.

// The five layers

What the model measures.

Every athlete receives five independent scores. The composite is a weighted average — but the shape of the five scores is the real signal. Two athletes can share a composite with completely different profiles.

1
Physical
Measurables, athleticism, size relative to position, speed, explosiveness. Sport-specific: 40-yard dash progression for football, vertical and wingspan for basketball, approach speed and arm speed for volleyball, pitch velocity and bat speed for baseball. Physical is the starting point — it is never the whole story.
Weight: 20% of composite · Can be corrected upward by other layers
2
Mindset ⬡ Crown layer
The most predictive and least-scored layer in the industry. Derived from: documented competitive behavior, injury response patterns, coaching feedback signals, academic profile, team leadership mentions, pressure-performance correlation, transfer pathway choices, and the narrative arc of how an athlete responds to adversity. JaMarcus Russell's Mindset was 5.5 — visible in the public record before the draft. Steph Curry's was 9.8. The market scored neither.
Weight: 25% of composite · The #1 predictor of professional success in the calibration corpus
3
Production
Output against competition, adjusted for context. Stat lines, tournament results, awards, and performance trajectory — but always weighted by who it was against. A 40-point game against elite competition scores differently than 40 against a weak schedule. The opportunity-context adjustment corrects for rural school, mid-major conference, and club access gaps systematically.
Weight: 25% of composite · Context-adjusted always
4
Defense
The most overlooked layer in recruiting. Derived from defensive stats where available, positioning mentions, coach descriptions of defensive IQ, and the willingness to guard elite opponents. Defensive specialists and two-way players receive a significant premium. The model specifically rewards athletes who embrace roles that don't generate highlight clips.
Weight: 15% of composite · Significantly underweighted by the market
5
Pathway
Where this athlete is going. Program fit, development environment, coach track record, pro pipeline probability, LOVB/NFL/NBA conversion rate at this composite tier, international opportunity signals, and the trajectory of athletes with similar profiles. Pathway is the forward-looking layer — it incorporates everything the other four layers predict about the ceiling.
Weight: 15% of composite · The pro probability engine
// Data sources

Where the evidence comes from.

FieldCheck reads the public record — not proprietary scouting networks, not paid access, not coach relationships. Every source is verifiable. Every claim is traceable.

The model explicitly flags when information is thin, when sources conflict, and when the Confidence Interval is wide. A wide CI is honest — it means the public record is incomplete, not that the athlete is unscoreable.

// The six constraints

What the model refuses to do.

The constraints are the moat. Any ranking system can score athletes. Not every system is willing to publish what it won't do.

1
No payola. Programs, coaches, agents, and players cannot pay to influence a FieldCheck IQ score. Scores are derived from the public record alone. This constraint is structural, not aspirational.
2
No coach preferences. The model does not weight a coach's opinion of their own player. Coach quotes are evidence of character signals — not performance evidence.
3
No ranking inheritance. A player ranked #1 by 247Sports does not start with a higher FC score. The model reads the evidence, not the existing ranking.
4
No club-access penalty. An athlete without elite club access doesn't receive a lower score for it. The opportunity-context adjustment corrects for access gaps — it doesn't penalize athletes for markets they weren't born into.
5
Mandatory confidence intervals. Every score publishes a CI. A score of 8.2 ± 0.8 is different from 8.2 ± 0.1. The width of the interval is itself a signal about how much public record evidence exists.
6
Contradiction transparency. When sources conflict, the model surfaces the conflict — it doesn't suppress it. A player whose stats contradict their reputation gets both the stats and the reputation, with confidence weights assigned to each.
