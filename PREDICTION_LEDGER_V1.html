The methodology · why this is harder than it looks

How a prediction enters this ledger

A FieldCheck verdict becomes a pre-registered prediction when three things happen simultaneously:

  1. The 25-PhD panel signs off on the v1.7 composite + CI + tier classification at a specific timestamp. Dissents (Vance dissented on Flagg) are documented in the audit trail but do not block lock.
  2. The verdict is frozen at lock · the FLAGG_V17-equivalent data structure is committed to source control · the immutable_until_checkin flag is set true · the next_checkin date is published with the verdict.
  3. The player's future-outcomes acquisition pipeline is wired · weekly outcome ingestion runs · the player joins the active cohort for that framework version.

Between lock and check-in, the verdict cannot be edited. New evidence (a great rookie season, an injury, a Character event) does not change the locked composite. We're scoring our own forecast, not chasing the news.

What counts as "wrong"

Wrong has three definitions, in increasing severity:

  1. Out-of-band wrong · the empirical 3-year composite falls outside the credible interval. For Flagg, this means his measured trajectory at Aug 15 2028 is below 8.46. If we said 9.30 ± 0.5 and reality returns 7.8, we were wrong, full stop.
  2. Wrong-tier wrong · the empirical tier is ≥2 levels below the predicted tier. ELITE+ predicted, MULTI-ALL-STAR delivered = within 1 tier (partial). ELITE+ predicted, SOLID-STARTER delivered = wrong-tier wrong.
  3. Cascade wrong · a categorical event (R6 activates) collapses the verdict. We treat this differently from continuous-trajectory misses because the framework anticipates these (Yamamoto's MIN function on Character is the structural defense). Documented with whether the cascade was predicted as a watch-list trigger.

The verdict closes "right" when the empirical 3-year composite falls inside the credible interval AND the empirical tier matches or exceeds the predicted tier.

Why we can do this · what Hudl, ESPN, and most evaluators can't

Most evaluators face a structural barrier to publishing failures: their business model depends on the appearance of authority. Hudl serves coaches buying for teams — admitting prediction failure undermines the procurement decision. ESPN is in the daily-content business — a 27-month-delayed grading exercise doesn't fit the publishing cadence. Most agencies have client-relationship conflicts that make public misses dangerous.

FieldCheck's position is different. The retail trust we sell is the only trust we have. If a verdict misses, the failure is the product — it teaches the framework what to refine in v1.8. Publishing the miss is a competitive moat, not a vulnerability.

The pricing model reinforces this. Free permanent · $29 Pro · $99 Edge · no affiliate revenue · no payola · walk-away default. There's no agency contract that breaks when we publish a miss. There's no team license that gets canceled. The user pays for verdict integrity, and verdict integrity requires the failures be visible.
